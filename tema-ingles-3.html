Master the tenses

Understanding different tenses allows you to express actions in the past, present experiences, and obligations clearly.

Past Continuous vs Past Simple

Actions in progress vs completed actions

was/werewhile/wheninterrupted

⏱️ Past Continuous vs Past Simple

The Past Simple expresses completed actions in the past. The Past Continuous (was/were + verb-ing) describes an action in progress at a specific moment in the past. They are often used together to show an interrupted action.

📌 Structure & Uses

  • Past Simple: Subject + V(past) — "She walked to school."
  • Past Continuous: Subject + was/were + V-ing — "She was walking to school."
  • Interrupted action: "I was reading when the phone rang."
  • Parallel actions: "She was cooking while he was sleeping."
  • Keywords: while, when, at that moment, at 8 o'clock yesterday

📝 Examples

  • "It was raining when I left the house."
  • "They were playing football when Maria arrived."
  • "While I was studying, my brother was watching TV."
  • Past Simple = short, completed action (the interruption)
  • Past Continuous = longer background action (what was happening)
was/were + V-ing (background) | V-past (interruption)

✏️ Exercises

  • Exercise 1 — Fill in the blanks Complete: "I _______ (study) when my friend _______ (call) me."
    "I was studying when my friend called me." — Past Continuous for the background action + Past Simple for the interruption.
  • Exercise 2 — Transform Rewrite using "while": "She cooked dinner. He fixed the car." (both at the same time)
    "While she was cooking dinner, he was fixing the car." or "She was cooking dinner while he was fixing the car."
  • Exercise 3 Write 2 sentences of your own using Past Continuous and Past Simple together.
    Sample answers: (1) "I was walking home when it started to rain." (2) "They were having lunch when the teacher entered the room."
✅

Present Perfect

have/has + past participle — experiences and recent actions

have/hassince/foralready/yet

✅ Present Perfect

The Present Perfect connects the past with the present. It is formed with have/has + past participle. It is used for experiences, recent actions, and situations that started in the past and continue now.

📌 Uses & Keywords

  • Experiences: "I have visited Paris." (sometime in life)
  • Recent actions: "She has just arrived."
  • Unfinished time: "We have lived here for 5 years."
  • Since: specific point — "since 2020", "since Monday"
  • For: duration — "for 3 years", "for a long time"
  • Already: (+) action done — "I have already eaten."
  • Yet: (?) / (−) — "Have you finished yet? / I haven't finished yet."
  • Just: very recently — "He has just left."

📝 Common Irregular Past Participles

  • go → gone | eat → eaten | see → seen
  • write → written | do → done | be → been
  • have → had | make → made | take → taken
  • come → come | run → run | buy → bought
  • speak → spoken | know → known | give → given
have/has + past participle | since (point) / for (duration)

✏️ Exercises

  • Exercise 1 — Fill in "She _______ (not/finish) her homework yet."
    "She has not finished / hasn't finished her homework yet."
  • Exercise 2 — Since or For? Choose: "I have studied English _______ three years." / "We have been friends _______ primary school."
    "...English for three years." (duration) / "...friends since primary school." (specific starting point)
  • Exercise 3 — Write sentences Write one sentence with "already" and one with "just".
    Samples: "I have already done my homework." / "She has just called me." (just = very recently)

Modal Verbs

can, must, should, may and their uses

can/couldmustshould

💬 Modal Verbs

Modal verbs are auxiliary verbs that add meaning to the main verb, expressing ability, permission, obligation, possibility, or advice. They are followed by the base form of the verb (infinitive without "to").

📌 Main Modal Verbs

  • can: ability — "I can swim."
  • could: past ability / polite request — "Could you help me?"
  • must: strong obligation — "You must wear a seatbelt."
  • have to: external obligation — "I have to go to school."
  • should / ought to: advice — "You should study more."
  • may: formal permission / possibility — "May I leave early?"
  • might: weak possibility — "It might rain tomorrow."

📝 Negatives & Questions

  • can't / cannot — "She can't drive."
  • mustn't — prohibition: "You mustn't smoke here."
  • don't have to — no obligation: "You don't have to come."
  • shouldn't — bad advice: "You shouldn't eat that."
  • Question: Modal + Subject + Verb? — "Can you swim?"
Subject + modal + base verb | must ≠ mustn't | have to ≠ don't have to

✏️ Exercises

  • Exercise 1 — Choose the correct modal "You _______ (obligation) eat vegetables every day." / "It _______ (possibility) snow tomorrow."
    "You should / must eat vegetables every day." / "It might / may snow tomorrow."
  • Exercise 2 What is the difference between "must" and "have to"? Give one example of each.
    Must: internal obligation or strong rule — "I must finish this report today." (my own decision/strong feeling). Have to: external obligation, rule from others — "I have to wear a uniform at school." (school rule)

Reported Speech básico

say/tell, pronoun changes and tense backshifting

say/tellbackshiftpronouns

🗣️ Reported Speech

Reported speech is used to tell what someone said without quoting them directly. The tense usually "backshifts" (goes one step back in time), and pronouns and time expressions change.

📌 Tense Backshifting

  • Present Simple → Past Simple
  • Present Continuous → Past Continuous
  • Past Simple → Past Perfect
  • will → would | can → could | may → might
  • Direct: "I am tired." → Reported: She said she was tired.
  • Direct: "I will call you." → She said she would call me.

📝 say vs tell

  • say: no object needed — "He said (that) he was hungry."
  • tell: needs an object (a person) — "He told me he was hungry."
  • Pronoun changes: I→he/she, we→they, my→his/her, you→I/he
  • Time: now→then, today→that day, tomorrow→the next day
He said (that) + reported clause | She told me (that) + reported clause

✏️ Exercises

  • Exercise 1 — Transform to Reported Speech "I love football." (John said...)
    John said (that) he loved football. (love → loved; I → he)
  • Exercise 2 "We will finish the project tomorrow." (They told us...)
    They told us (that) they would finish the project the next day. (will→would; we→they; tomorrow→the next day)

Essay Structure

Introduction, body paragraphs and conclusion

ThesisTopic sentenceConclusion

📄 Essay Writing Structure

A well-structured essay in English has three main parts: introduction, body, and conclusion. Each part has a specific function and helps communicate your ideas clearly and logically.

📌 Essay Structure

  • Introduction: Hook (attention-grabber) + Background + Thesis statement (main argument)
  • Body Paragraph 1: Topic sentence + Evidence/Example + Explanation
  • Body Paragraph 2: Topic sentence + Evidence/Example + Explanation
  • Conclusion: Restate thesis + Summary of points + Closing thought

📝 Useful Linking Words

  • Adding: Furthermore, Moreover, In addition, Also
  • Contrasting: However, On the other hand, Although, Despite
  • Cause/Effect: Therefore, As a result, Consequently, Because of
  • Conclusion: In conclusion, To summarize, Overall
Introduction → Body (2-3 paragraphs) → Conclusion

✏️ Exercises

  • Exercise 1 — Identify the parts Label: (a) "In conclusion, social media has both benefits and drawbacks." (b) "Social media has transformed how we communicate." (c) "Firstly, it allows people to connect instantly worldwide."
    (a) Conclusion sentence (b) Thesis statement / Introduction (c) Topic sentence of body paragraph 1
  • Exercise 2 — Creative writing Write a short introduction (3-4 sentences) for the topic: "Technology in education."
    Sample: "Technology has become an essential part of our daily lives. In recent years, schools around the world have begun using digital tools to improve learning. However, the impact of technology on education is a topic of great debate. This essay will argue that technology, when used correctly, greatly enhances the learning experience." (last sentence = thesis)
